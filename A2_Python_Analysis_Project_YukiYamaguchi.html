Introduction

An exploratory analysis of one year of bike rental data reveals clear usage patterns, including both regular and occasional use. Among regular users, commuting and short-distance daytime travel such as shopping are the two main purposes.

After careful data preprocessing, such as mindful of statistical pitfalls like outliers, low data counts in specific weather conditions, and multicollinearity, optimal weather conditions for high rental activity were identified.

Based on these findings, we propose four actionable recommendations to support revenue maximization for operators.

Key Insights

1. User Segments (Use Cases)

  • Bike rental usage can be categorized into three main patterns:
    1. Weekday commuting, especially Monday through Thursday
    (Friday shows a noticeable dip, likely due to remote work). 2. Midday trips for shopping or errands, steady across all weekdays. 3. Weekend leisure usage, with temporary spikes for recreation.

2. Ideal Weather Conditions for High Demand

  • Our analysis shows that rentals significantly increase under the following weather conditions:
    1. Sunny skies (no rainfall)
    2. Higher temperatures, Lower humidity, Higher Wind Speed, Lower UV Index (Visibility is not that relevant)
    3. Moderate Wind speed between 2 and 3 m/s

How do ideal weather conditions influence rentals?

I analyzed which weather conditions contribute most to maximizing bike rental volume. While the full statistical details are omitted here, the following analytical steps were taken after data preprocessing:

  1. Compare Average Rentals on Rainy vs. Non-Rainy Days
    A simple comparison of mean rental counts was performed between days with and without precipitation to quantify the impact of weather conditions on demand. (Wang & Akar, 2015)

  2. Conduct Multiple Linear Regression
    We used Temperature, Humidity, Wind Speed, Visibility, and UV Index as explanatory variables and evaluated both the p-values and regression coefficients to identify which factors significantly influence rental volume.

  3. Histogram Analysis of Wind Speed
    Wind speed was grouped into 1-unit bins, and a histogram of average rentals was created to visually determine the optimal wind speed range for maximizing rentals.

Strategic Recommendations

1. General Operations

  • Schedule recharging and bike maintenance during early morning hours when demand is low.
  • On days with favorable weather, allocate more bikes to expected high-demand areas.

2. Cluster 1: Weekday Commuters

  • On weekday mornings, redistribute bikes from downtown areas to residential zones and transit hubs so commuters easily find available bikes.
  • After weekends, move bikes from downtown (likely used for weekend leisure) back to residential areas.

3. Cluster 2: Daytime Shoppers

  • Partner with grocery stores and apparel brands to offer coupon-based incentives (e.g., cashback via QR on receipts).

4. Cluster 1 & 2: Subscription Strategy

  • Launch a weekday-only subscription plan, targeting commuters and errand users.
(Anodot, n.d.)

5. Cluster 3: Weekend Leisure Users

  • Collaborate with entertainment facilities or outlets to create weekend stamp-rally promotions, offering cashback for multi-stop leisure rides.

Conclusion

This analysis reveals that bike rental demand is driven by a combination of user purpose, time of day, and weather conditions.

To capitalize on these insights, operators should not only optimize operational efficiency for existing users such as using early morning hours for strategic recharging and bike redistribution, but also consider growth strategies aimed at expanding the user base like partnering with retail and entertainment businesses to offer targeted promotions, leveraging weekday routines to attract commuter and errand-based users. And introducing flexible subscription models tailored to distinct user segments will work too.

Aligning services with real-world behavior will enable smarter fleet management and long-term demand generation.

import pandas as pd
import seaborn as sns
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

# Definine time range based on general life rhythm
def classify_time_period(hour):
    if 4 <= hour < 7:
        return 'Early Morning'
    elif 7 <= hour < 9:
        return 'Morning Commute'
    elif 9 <= hour < 16:
        return 'Midday'
    elif 16 <= hour < 19:
        return 'Evening Commute'
    elif 19 <= hour < 23:
        return 'Night'
    else:
        return 'Mid Night'

# add a new column, time_slot
filtered_df['Time Period'] = filtered_df['Hour'].apply(classify_time_period)

# Calculate mean of RENTALS based on time period and day of the week
pivot_table = filtered_df.pivot_table(
    index='Time Period',
    columns='Day of Week',
    values='RENTALS',
    aggfunc='mean'
)

# Clarify the order of day of week
day_order = ['Monday', 'Tuesday', 'Wednesday', 'Thursday', 'Friday', 'Saturday', 'Sunday']
pivot_table = pivot_table[day_order]
# Clarify the order of time period
time_order = ['Early Morning', 'Morning Commute', 'Midday', 'Evening Commute', 'Night', 'Mid Night']
pivot_table = pivot_table.reindex(time_order)

# define a function to draw a heatmap
def draw_heatmap():
    plt.figure(figsize=(10, 6))
    sns.heatmap(pivot_table, annot=True, fmt=".0f", cmap='Blues')
    plt.title("Average Rentals by Time Period and Day of Week")
    plt.ylabel("Time Period")
    plt.xlabel("Day of Week")
    plt.tight_layout()
    plt.show()

import pandas as pd
import seaborn as sns
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

# add a flag column, rain or no-rain
df['Rain_or_Not'] = df['Rainfall'].apply(lambda x: 'Rain' if x > 0 else 'No Rain')

# calculate mean of rentals of each group
avg_rentals = df.groupby('Rain_or_Not')['RENTALS'].mean().reset_index()

# create a bar graph
def draw_bar_graph_rain():
    plt.figure(figsize=(6, 4))
    sns.barplot(data=avg_rentals, x='Rain_or_Not', y='RENTALS', palette=['skyblue', 'gray'])

    plt.title('Average Rentals on Rainy vs. Non-Rainy Days')
    plt.xlabel('Weather Condition')
    plt.ylabel('Average Number of Rentals')
